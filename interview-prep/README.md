## 🎯 How to Use These Guides

### Step 1: Review Questions
Start with the **questions.md** file for the relevant discipline. Read through all questions to understand the scope of topics you should be prepared to discuss.

### Step 2: Study Example Answers
Use the **ANSWERS.md** file to:
- See how to structure answers using STAR format (Situation → Task → Action → Result)
- Learn what makes answers strong (specificity, technical depth, collaboration examples)
- Understand common pitfalls to avoid (vague responses, blaming others, skipping outcomes)

### Step 3: Write Your Own Answers
For the most important questions, write out your own answers:
- "Tell us about yourself"
- "Why [Company Name]?" (requires research first)
- "Why are you leaving [current employer]?"
- 2-3 project STAR stories (your most impressive/relevant projects)
- 2-3 behavioral STAR stories (challenges, mistakes, problem-solving)

### Step 4: Practice Out Loud
- Don't just read—speak your answers conversationally
- Record yourself and listen for clarity, confidence, and conciseness
- Time your answers (30-60 sec for intro, 2-3 min max for STAR stories)
- Practice until answers feel natural, not memorized

### Step 5: Mock Interview
- Have someone randomly ask questions from the list
- Practice answering without notes
- Get feedback on technical accuracy, clarity, and delivery
- Practice handling follow-up questions

### Step 6: Research the Company
Before the interview:
- Study their recent projects and portfolio
- Review leadership team profiles (LinkedIn)
- Read Glassdoor reviews and LinkedIn posts
- Understand their market position and specialties
- Prepare 5-7 thoughtful questions to ask them

## 💡 Universal Interview Tips

### The 3-Second Rule
Pause 2-3 seconds after they finish asking before answering. This prevents interrupting, gives you time to think, and makes you appear thoughtful.

### The 2-Minute Rule
Keep most answers to 60-120 seconds. If you go longer, you risk losing their attention. Wrap up and offer: *"Happy to go deeper on any of that if useful."*

### The Specificity Principle
Specific details beat generic claims every time:
- ❌ "I worked on many projects"
- ✅ "I designed plumbing for a 200-bed hospital, a 15-story office tower, and a biotech research lab"

### The Positivity Filter
Stay constructive, even when discussing challenges:
- ❌ "My last project was a disaster because the architect was incompetent"
- ✅ "One challenging project involved frequent design changes, which taught me to build more frequent coordination checkpoints"

### The Confidence Calibration
- Don't hedge on things you know: "IPC requires..." not "I think IPC requires..."
- DO acknowledge what you don't know: "I haven't designed that system yet, but it's something I'm eager to learn"

## 🔧 Discipline-Specific Notes

### For Plumbing Engineering Interviews:
- Emphasize **systems thinking** (water, drainage, venting, gas all interact)
- Show **code fluency** (IPC/UPC, IBC, NFPA 99 for healthcare)
- Demonstrate **coordination skills** (plumbing touches every discipline)
- Highlight **sustainability** if the firm does green buildings (LEED, WaterSense, alternative water systems)
- Mention **energy efficiency** (hot water systems, insulation, controls)

### For Fire Protection Design Interviews:
- Emphasize **life safety mindset** (zero tolerance for failure)
- Show **code expertise** (NFPA 13, 14, 20, 72 are your daily references)
- Demonstrate **hydraulic thinking** (pressure, flow, remote area, friction loss)
- Highlight **AHJ navigation** skills (code interpretations, equivalencies, first-pass approvals)
- Mention **performance-based design** if the firm does advanced work
