# Plumbing Engineer Interview Prep – Complete Question Set

This comprehensive guide covers technical, behavioral, and fit questions for Plumbing Engineer interviews.

---

## **Part 1: Opening & Background**

### **1) Tell us about yourself.**

**Framework:** 30–60 seconds covering background → technical interests → why plumbing engineering

**Key elements:**
- Engineering family background (mechanical + architecture exposure)
- Early technical curiosity (Hoffmans apparatus, solar modeling, etc.)
- Connection to plumbing/building systems (mechanical engineering foundation)
- Current role summary and relevant experience

**Example structure:**  
*"I grew up in Tampa in an engineering family—father's a mechanical engineer, mother's an architect. I got interested in building systems early and studied Mechanical Engineering at USF, focusing on fluid mechanics and thermodynamics. I was drawn to plumbing engineering because it combines hydraulic design, energy efficiency, and practical problem-solving—systems that are critical to building function but require creative solutions within tight constraints. At [current employer], I've been working on [domestic water, drainage, gas systems] for [commercial/institutional/healthcare] projects, which has given me solid code knowledge and hands-on coordination experience."*

- Historic renovation (working within existing constraints, code equivalencies)

## **Part 3: Project Experience & Technical Questions**

### **5) Please tell us about your project experience, starting with the most relevant experience.**

**Prepare 2–3 project stories** (most complex, most recent, most relevant to the target firm)

**Use STAR format:**
- **Situation:** Building type, client, context
- **Task:** Your role, what needed to be accomplished
- **Action:** Specific steps you took, technical decisions made
- **Result:** Outcome, metrics, lessons learned

**Include:** building type, scope, your role, deliverables, challenges, outcome

### **Technical Design Questions**

### **6) Walk me through how you'd design a domestic water system for a multi-story office building.**

**Key steps to cover:**
1. Determine fixture counts and types based on occupancy and building codes
2. Calculate water demand using fixture units (IPC/UPC methods)
3. Size water service and building main
4. Determine if pressure zoning or booster pumps are needed
5. Layout risers, branches, and fixture rough-ins
6. Select pipe materials and insulation requirements
7. Coordinate with architectural, structural, and other MEP disciplines
8. Prepare permit submittal documents

### **7) How do you calculate domestic water demand and size the water service?**

**Cover:**
- Fixture unit method (IPC Table 610.3 or equivalent)
- Conversion from fixture units to GPM using code tables
- Peak demand considerations vs. average demand
- Pressure requirements at fixtures (minimum 20-30 psi residual)
- Friction loss calculations for pipe sizing
- Available water pressure from utility (static and residual)
- Need for booster pumps or pressure-reducing valves

### **8) How do you approach pressure zoning in a high-rise building?**

**Cover:**
- Maximum allowable pressure at fixtures (typically 80 psi)
- Zone break calculations based on building height
- Pressure-reducing valves (PRVs) vs separate booster systems
- Advantages/disadvantages of each approach
- Coordination with domestic hot water system
- Backflow prevention requirements at zone boundaries

### **9) What factors do you consider when selecting pipe materials?**

**Cover:**
- Application (domestic cold/hot water, drainage, vent, gas, medical gas)
- Code requirements and approvals
- Water chemistry and corrosion potential
- Pressure and temperature ratings
- Installation environment (concealed vs exposed, indoor vs outdoor)
- Cost and availability
- Maintenance and longevity
- Common materials: copper (Type K/L/M), PEX, CPVC, PVC, HDPE, ductile iron, stainless steel

### **10) Walk me through sanitary drainage and vent system design.**

**Cover:**
- Fixture drainage load calculations (drainage fixture units)
- Horizontal drain sizing (slope requirements, 1/4" per foot typical)
- Vertical stack sizing (considering offset stacks)
- Vent system design (individual vents, common vents, wet vents, stack vents)
- Vent termination requirements (distance from air intakes, roof penetrations)
- Cleanout locations and access requirements
- Special considerations (grease interceptors, oil/sand separators, backwater valves)

### **11) How do you size a sanitary waste stack?**

**Cover:**
- Accumulate drainage fixture units (DFUs) from all floors
- Apply code tables (IPC Table 710.1(2) or equivalent)
- Consider stack offsets and their impact on capacity
- Horizontal drain sizing at base of stack
- Building drain and building sewer sizing
- Connection to public sewer or private treatment

### **12) What are the key considerations for medical gas system design?**

**Cover:**
- Types of medical gases (oxygen, medical air, nitrous oxide, nitrogen, vacuum, WAGD)
- NFPA 99 requirements and compliance
- Source equipment (manifolds, compressors, vacuum pumps)
- Zone valve boxes and alarm requirements
- Pipe materials (copper Type K or L, brazed joints)
- Testing and certification requirements
- Coordination with electrical for alarms and monitoring

### **13) How do you coordinate plumbing systems with other trades?**

**Cover:**
- BIM coordination and clash detection (Navisworks)
- Structural coordination (core penetrations, floor drains, roof drains, pipe support loads)
- Architectural coordination (fixture locations, access panels, chases, room layouts)
- MEP coordination (shared chases, clearances, priority/hierarchy)
- Fire protection coordination (backflow preventers, water service, isolation valves)
- Early coordination to avoid field conflicts

### **14) What do you check before finalizing plumbing design documents?**

**Cover:**
- Code compliance verification (fixture counts, accessibility, materials, sizing)
- Calculation review (water demand, drainage loads, gas loads, pump sizing)
- Fixture schedule and specification accuracy
- Riser diagram completeness and accuracy
- Detail and section cross-references
- Clash detection resolution
- Coordination with specifications
- Permit submittal requirements

## **Part 4: Code & Standards**

### **15) Which codes and standards do you use most often?**

**List key ones:**
- **IPC** (International Plumbing Code) or **UPC** (Uniform Plumbing Code) depending on jurisdiction
- **IBC** (International Building Code) – occupancy, accessibility, fixture counts
- **NFPA 99** (Healthcare Facilities Code) – medical gas systems
- **ASME A112** series – plumbing fixtures and materials standards
- **ASHRAE 90.1 / Florida Energy Code** – water heating efficiency
- **ADA / ICC A117.1** – accessibility requirements
- **Local amendments** (Florida Building Code, county/city plumbing codes)

### **16) How do you stay current on code changes?**

**Good answers:**
- Track code adoption cycles (IPC/UPC/IBC typically on 3-year cycle)
- Attend continuing education courses and webinars
- Participate in professional organizations (ASPE, ASHRAE, IAPMO, ICC)
- Subscribe to code update newsletters and industry publications
- Internal firm training when new code editions are adopted
- Maintain reference library with current codes and local amendments

### **17) Can you describe a time local code requirements differed from baseline IPC/UPC?**

**Use STAR format with specific example**

### **18) How do you handle accessibility requirements (ADA/FBC) in plumbing design?**

**Cover:**
- Fixture counts for accessible fixtures (water closets, lavatories, bathing)
- Clearance and reach range requirements
- Mounting heights and operating controls
- Accessible routes to plumbing fixtures
- Grab bar blocking and reinforcement coordination
- Coordination with architect on room layouts

### **19) What documentation do you prepare for permit submittals?**

**Cover:**
- Plumbing plans (floor plans showing all fixtures, piping, equipment)
- Riser diagrams (domestic water, hot water, drainage, vent, gas)
- Equipment schedules (pumps, water heaters, backflow preventers)
- Fixture schedules
- Plumbing calculations (water demand, drainage loads, gas sizing)
- Product data sheets and specifications
- Code compliance narrative or matrix

## **Part 5: Behavioral & Problem-Solving**

### **20) Tell us about a challenge and how you dealt with it.**

**Use STAR format**

**Best story types:**
- Solved unexpected site condition (inadequate water pressure, existing infrastructure conflicts)
- Met tight deadline with design changes
- Navigated complex code interpretation with AHJ
- Resolved coordination conflict between trades
- Caught major error before construction
- Adapted design to budget constraints

### **21) Describe a time you caught a major error before installation.**

**What to include:**
- What the error was (sizing mistake, code violation, coordination conflict)
- How you discovered it (QA review, coordination meeting, site visit)
- Impact if it had gone unnoticed (cost, schedule, code compliance, functionality)
- How you corrected it
- Process improvements implemented afterward

### **22) How do you prioritize when multiple projects are due at once?**

**Cover:**
- Deadline mapping and working backward
- Communication with project managers
- Task batching for efficiency
- Early escalation when overloaded
- Time management tools and techniques

### **23) How do you handle disagreements with architects, contractors, or inspectors?**

**Cover:**
- Active listening and understanding their perspective
- Code or technical basis for your position
- Finding common ground and alternative solutions
- Escalation path when needed
- Documentation of decisions and agreements

### **24) Tell me about a project that went wrong—what did you learn?**

**Be honest but constructive:**
- What happened (be specific)
- Your role and responsibility
- Impact on project (cost, schedule, client satisfaction)
- What you learned
- How you've applied that lesson since
- Process improvements you implemented

## **Part 6: Collaboration & Workflow**

### **25) How do you work within a team?**

**Include examples of:**
- Coordinating with architects and other engineers (structural, mechanical, electrical, FP)
- Supporting or mentoring junior designers
- Collaborating with project managers on scope and schedule
- Working with contractors and manufacturers
- Escalating issues appropriately
- Contributing to team knowledge sharing

### **26) What software and BIM tools have you used?**

**List specific tools and proficiency level:**
- **Revit MEP** (plumbing system modeling, fixture families)
- **AutoCAD MEP** or **AutoCAD** (2D design)
- **Navisworks** (clash detection and coordination)
- **Pipe sizing / calculation tools** (pipe friction loss, pump selection)
- **Elite Software** or similar (load calculations)
- **Bluebeam** (markup and review)
- **Microsoft Project** or other scheduling tools

### **27) How do you perform clash detection and resolve coordination issues?**

**Cover:**
- BIM coordination process and meetings
- Navisworks or similar clash detection tools
- Prioritization of clashes (hard clashes vs. clearance issues)
- Trade hierarchy and coordination responsibility
- When plumbing can/should adjust vs. when to advocate
- Documentation of resolutions
- Model updates to reflect agreed solutions

### **28) How do you QA/QC your drawings before issue?**

**Cover:**
- Internal checklists (code compliance, calculations, standards)
- Self-review (cross-referencing plans, details, schedules, specs)
- Peer review process
- Senior engineer or PE review
- Calculation verification (independent check or software validation)
- Coordination verification (clash-free models)

### **29) What is your process for responding to RFIs and construction administration?**

**Cover:**
- RFI tracking system (log, response time commitments)
- Coordination with design team and PM
- Site visit observations and field reports
- Submittal review (equipment, fixtures, materials)
- Shop drawing review and approval process
- Punch list and closeout coordination

## **Part 7: Energy Efficiency & Sustainability**

### **30) How do you approach water efficiency and sustainable plumbing design?**

**Cover:**
- Low-flow fixtures (WaterSense labeled)
- High-efficiency water heaters (tankless, heat pump, solar thermal)
- Rainwater harvesting and graywater reuse systems
- Hot water recirculation strategies (demand vs continuous, controls)
- Insulation and heat trace for energy conservation
- LEED or other green building certification requirements

### **31) What experience do you have with alternative water systems (rainwater, graywater)?**

**Cover:**
- System components (collection, filtration, storage, distribution, treatment)
- Code requirements and permitting (IPC Appendix C, local amendments)
- Water quality and treatment needs
- Cross-connection control and backflow prevention
- Maintenance and operational considerations
- Payback analysis and lifecycle cost

## **Part 8: Specialized Systems**

### **32) What is your experience with natural gas or fuel gas systems?**

**Cover:**
- Gas load calculations (Btu/h from appliances)
- Pipe sizing methods (longest length method, branch length method)
- Pipe materials (black steel, CSST, PE for underground)
- Pressure drop calculations
- Regulator and meter sizing
- Ventilation requirements for gas equipment
- Code compliance (IFGC or local gas code)

### **33) Have you designed domestic hot water systems? Walk me through your approach.**

**Cover:**
- Hot water demand calculation (fixture units, GPH, or GPM methods)
- Water heater selection (tank, tankless, boiler with storage, heat pump, solar)
- Temperature requirements (120°F domestic, 140°F+ for some applications, anti-scald considerations)
- Recirculation system design (pumps, controls, balancing valves, insulation)
- Expansion tanks and pressure relief
- Energy efficiency considerations
- Legionella prevention (temperature maintenance, dead legs, flushing)

### **34) What do you know about backflow prevention and cross-connection control?**

**Cover:**
- Hazard classification (health hazard vs non-health, high hazard vs low)
- Backflow preventer types (RP, DC, PVB, AVB, DCVA)
- Application and selection criteria
- Testing and maintenance requirements
- Location and installation considerations (drainage, freeze protection, accessibility)
- Local water utility requirements

## **Part 9: Questions for Them**

### **35) What questions do you have for us?**

**Strong questions to ask:**

**About the work:**
- What types of projects are most common here (commercial, healthcare, institutional, industrial)?
- What's the typical project size and complexity?
- Are there opportunities to work on specialized systems (medical gas, laboratory, high-rise)?
- How does the firm approach sustainable design and LEED projects?

**About the role:**
- How much design autonomy does this role have vs senior engineer or PM oversight?
- What does success look like in the first 90 days?
- What's the typical project team structure?
- How is work assigned and balanced across the team?

**About tools and development:**
- What software platform does the firm use (Revit, AutoCAD, other)?
- Is the firm fully on BIM, or hybrid 2D/3D?
- How do you support continuing education and PE licensure?
- Are there opportunities for ASPE involvement or other professional development?

**About culture and growth:**
- How would you describe the team culture and collaboration style?
- What do you enjoy most about working here?
- How does the firm support work-life balance?
- What does the career path look like from this position?
- How does the firm support professional registration (PE, CPD, LEED AP)?

## **Action Plan for Interview Prep**

### **Step 1: Write Your Answers**
Create detailed written answers for:
- "Tell us about yourself"
- "Why [Company Name]?" (research first)
- "Why leave [current employer]?"
- 2–3 project STAR stories (most impressive/relevant projects)
- 2–3 challenge/problem-solving STAR stories

### **Step 2: Practice Out Loud**
- Don't just read—speak answers conversationally
- Record yourself and listen back
- Time your responses (30-60 sec for intro, 2-3 min for STAR stories)
- Practice until answers feel natural, not memorized

### **Step 3: Mock Interview**
- Have someone ask questions randomly from this list
- Practice answering without notes
- Get feedback on clarity, confidence, technical accuracy
- Practice handling follow-up questions

### **Step 4: Research the Company Thoroughly**
- Recent projects (portfolio, case studies, awards)
- Leadership team and key staff (LinkedIn)
- Company culture (Glassdoor, LinkedIn posts)
- Industry reputation and market position
- Office locations and project geography
- Specialized capabilities or unique approaches

### **Step 5: Prepare Your Questions**
- Select 5–7 questions from Part 9 that genuinely interest you
- Prioritize based on what you learn during the interview
- Have backup questions in case some are answered naturally

### **Step 6: Logistics**
- Confirm interview time, location, format (in-person/virtual)
- Test technology if virtual (Zoom, Teams, etc.)
- Prepare professional background for virtual interviews
- Bring copies of resume, portfolio/project list, and references
- Arrive 10-15 minutes early (in-person) or log in 5 minutes early (virtual)

---

## **Final Tips**

**Do:**
- Be authentic and enthusiastic about plumbing engineering
- Show continuous learning mindset and adaptability
- Demonstrate systems thinking (how plumbing integrates with other disciplines)
- Ask thoughtful questions that show you've researched the firm
- Follow up with thank-you notes within 24 hours

**Don't:**
- Badmouth current or previous employers
- Claim expertise in areas you haven't worked in (be honest about what you're eager to learn)
- Forget to research the company
- Skip preparing questions for them
- Forget to bring work samples or project list if requested
