# Plumbing Engineer Interview Prep – Example Answers with Commentary

This companion guide provides model answers for the questions in `plumbing-engineer-questions.md`, with analysis of what makes each answer strong and common pitfalls to avoid.

## **Part 1: Opening & Background**

### **1) Tell us about yourself.**

**Example Answer:**

*"I grew up in Tampa in an engineering family—my father is a mechanical engineer and my mother is an architect—so I was exposed to building systems and design thinking from an early age. I got hooked on technical problem-solving in middle school when I built a solar thermal system for a science fair project that won statewide recognition.*

*I studied Mechanical Engineering at USF, where I focused on fluid mechanics and thermodynamics—the foundation of plumbing system design. During an internship, I worked on the MEP coordination for a hospital project, and I realized that plumbing systems were the most interesting to me because they combine hydraulic design, energy efficiency, and practical constraints. You're designing critical infrastructure that has to work flawlessly but often gets the least space and budget.*

*After graduating, I joined [current employer] as a Plumbing Designer, where I've spent the past [X years] designing domestic water, sanitary, storm drainage, and gas systems for commercial, institutional, and healthcare projects. I've worked on everything from office buildings to hospitals to laboratories, which has given me solid experience with IPC/UPC codes, hydraulic calculations, BIM coordination, and navigating complex AHJ requirements. I'm looking to take the next step with a firm that specializes in complex, high-performance plumbing design, which is what brought me here."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Concise** (~60-75 seconds spoken)
- ✅ **Narrative arc**: background → education → interest origin → current role → why this opportunity
- ✅ **Specific examples**: solar project, hospital internship, project types
- ✅ **Shows genuine interest**: "most interesting... combine hydraulic design, energy efficiency, practical constraints"
- ✅ **Demonstrates understanding** of plumbing's challenges and value
- ✅ **Forward-looking**: ends with why this firm

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Generic opening ("I'm a hard worker")
- ❌ Rambling unrelated childhood stories
- ❌ Going over 90 seconds
- ❌ Just reciting resume bullets
- ❌ Not connecting education/background to plumbing specifically

*My role was lead plumbing designer. The DI water system tested at the required resistivity on first commissioning."*

### **6) Walk me through how you'd design a domestic water system for a multi-story office building.**

**Example Answer:**

*"I'd start by determining the fixture count based on the occupancy and building codes—IBC tells me how many water closets, lavatories, and drinking fountains I need based on the occupant load and use type. Then I lay out the fixture locations in coordination with the architect's floor plans.*

*Next, I calculate the water demand using the fixture unit method from the IPC. I assign fixture units to each fixture type, total them up, and convert to gallons per minute using the code tables. I also check the available water pressure from the utility—if I have good street pressure and the building is low-rise, I might be able to serve it directly. But for anything over 4-5 stories, I'm probably looking at a booster pump system or pressure zoning.*

*For a typical mid-rise office, I'd design a domestic water booster system in the basement or ground floor mechanical room—duplex pumps with VFDs, hydropneumatic tank, and controls. I'd route risers vertically through plumbing chases, with horizontal branches on each floor to serve the restrooms and break rooms. I'd also coordinate with the architect on water heater locations and size the hot water system based on demand.*

*Throughout the process, I'm coordinating with structural for pipe penetrations and support loads, mechanical for shared chases and clearances, and fire protection because they're typically pulling from the same water service, so I need to make sure we have adequate flow and pressure for both systems.*

*Finally, I prepare the permit submittal—floor plans, riser diagrams, equipment schedules, water demand calculations, and specifications."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Logical sequence**: fixture count → demand calculation → pressure analysis → system design → coordination → submittal
- ✅ **Shows code knowledge**: IBC occupancy loads, IPC fixture units
- ✅ **Demonstrates engineering judgment**: "over 4-5 stories, probably looking at booster pumps"
- ✅ **Practical awareness**: mentions coordination realities (structural, mechanical, FP)
- ✅ **Complete process**: doesn't stop at design—includes permitting

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Skipping fixture count determination (first step, driven by code)
- ❌ Ignoring available water pressure (critical design input)
- ❌ Designing in isolation (plumbing must coordinate)
- ❌ Overloading with jargon without explaining flow

### **7) How do you calculate domestic water demand and size the water service?**

**Example Answer:**

*"I use the fixture unit method from the IPC. I assign water supply fixture units to each fixture—for example, a water closet is typically 2.5 WSFU for a flush tank type, a lavatory is 1.0 WSFU, a drinking fountain is 0.5 WSFU. I total up all the fixture units for the entire building.*

*Then I convert fixture units to gallons per minute using the IPC demand table—Table 610.3. The table accounts for the fact that not all fixtures are running simultaneously, so there's a built-in diversity factor. For example, 100 fixture units converts to about 42 GPM.*

*Once I have the demand in GPM, I check the available water pressure from the utility. Ideally I get a fire flow test showing static pressure, residual pressure at a certain flow rate, and the size of the water main. I need to make sure I have at least 20-30 psi residual pressure at the highest and most remote fixture after accounting for friction loss, elevation loss, and any pressure drop through the meter and backflow preventer.*

*If the available pressure isn't adequate, I either upsize the service to reduce friction loss, or I design a booster pump system to make up the deficit. I also coordinate with fire protection early because they typically have a much larger water demand, and we often share the same water service."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Specific method**: IPC fixture unit tables (with example numbers)
- ✅ **Explains diversity**: notes that table accounts for simultaneous use factor
- ✅ **Shows complete analysis**: demand calculation → available pressure → friction/elevation loss → adequacy check
- ✅ **Practical awareness**: mentions coordination with fire protection (shared service)
- ✅ **Demonstrates problem-solving**: upsizing or booster pumps if pressure inadequate

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Vague answer without numbers or method
- ❌ Forgetting to account for available pressure (critical input)
- ❌ Skipping friction loss and elevation loss
- ❌ Not mentioning fire protection coordination

### **10) Walk me through sanitary drainage and vent system design.**

**Example Answer:**

*"I start by assigning drainage fixture units to each plumbing fixture—the IPC has tables that give DFU values based on fixture type. For example, a water closet is typically 4 DFUs for drainage, a lavatory is 1 DFU, a floor drain might be 2 DFUs.*

*Next, I lay out the drainage piping, starting from the fixtures and working downstream. Horizontal drains need slope—typically 1/4 inch per foot for pipes up to 3 inches, and 1/8 inch per foot for larger pipes. I size the horizontal branches and mains using the IPC drainage tables based on the accumulated DFUs.*

*For vertical stacks, I total up all the DFUs from floors above and size the stack accordingly. I pay attention to offsets—if a stack has an offset more than 45 degrees, it affects the capacity and I need to treat it carefully per code.*

*On the vent side, I design individual vents for each fixture trap when possible, but I also use common vents, wet vents, and stack venting where code allows—it can save pipe and make the system more compact. Vents have to terminate outdoors, typically through the roof, at least 6 inches above the roofline and a certain distance from any air intakes or windows.*

*I also locate cleanouts at changes of direction and at specified intervals so the system is maintainable. And for commercial kitchens I coordinate with the owner on grease interceptors, and for parking garages I sometimes need oil/sand separators or backwater valves to prevent flooding."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Logical sequence**: fixture units → horizontal drains → vertical stacks → vents → accessories
- ✅ **Shows code knowledge**: DFU values, slope requirements, offset impacts, vent termination
- ✅ **Demonstrates design efficiency**: mentions common vents, wet vents (not just individual vents)
- ✅ **Practical details**: cleanouts, grease interceptors, oil/sand separators
- ✅ **Complete system thinking**: drainage + venting + maintenance access

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Ignoring vent system (common mistake—drainage alone doesn't work)
- ❌ Not mentioning slope requirements (critical for gravity drainage)
- ❌ Skipping cleanout locations (code requirement and maintenance need)
- ❌ Forgetting specialty drainage (grease, oil/sand)

### **12) What are the key considerations for medical gas system design?**

**Example Answer:**

*"Medical gas systems are governed by NFPA 99, which is the healthcare facilities code. The most common gases are oxygen, medical air, nitrous oxide, nitrogen, vacuum, and WAGD—waste anesthetic gas disposal.*

*The design starts with understanding the clinical needs—how many operating rooms, patient rooms, procedure spaces, and what gas outlets are required in each. Each outlet has to be readily accessible and within reach of the patient bed or procedure location.*

*Source equipment is critical—oxygen and nitrous are typically supplied from manifolds fed by liquid or compressed gas cylinders, with automatic changeover and alarm systems. Medical air comes from dedicated oil-free compressors with dryers and filtration. Vacuum is generated by duplex or triplex vacuum pumps with receivers.*

*Piping is almost always copper—Type K or Type L with brazed joints, no soldering or threading. The system has to be cleaned and pressure-tested to very tight standards before it's put into service. We also design zone valve boxes on each floor so sections can be isolated for maintenance without shutting down the whole hospital.*

*Alarms are a big part of the design—area alarms and master alarms that monitor pressure, flow, and source equipment status. I coordinate closely with electrical on the alarm panel locations and wiring. And every system has to be inspected and certified by a qualified agency before occupancy."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Starts with code reference**: NFPA 99
- ✅ **Lists key gases**: oxygen, medical air, nitrous, nitrogen, vacuum, WAGD
- ✅ **Explains source equipment**: manifolds, compressors, vacuum pumps (shows system-level thinking)
- ✅ **Mentions critical details**: Type K/L copper, brazed joints, cleaning, zone valves, alarms
- ✅ **Demonstrates coordination**: electrical (alarms), certification requirements

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Vague answer ("I know medical gas is important")
- ❌ Missing NFPA 99 reference (the governing code)
- ❌ Ignoring source equipment (not just piping)
- ❌ Forgetting alarms and zone valves (critical safety features)

## **Part 4: Code & Standards**

### **15) Which codes and standards do you use most often?**

**Example Answer:**

*"On almost every project, I'm working with the International Plumbing Code—that's my primary reference for fixture counts, drainage and vent sizing, water distribution, and materials. In Florida specifically, we're using the Florida Building Code which is based on the IBC and IPC with state amendments.*

*For healthcare projects, NFPA 99 is critical—it governs medical gas systems, vacuum, clinical sinks, and other healthcare-specific requirements. I also reference the IBC heavily for occupancy classifications, occupant loads, and accessibility requirements, which drive my fixture counts and types.*

*For accessibility, I use the ADA and ICC A117.1 to make sure fixture clearances, mounting heights, grab bars, and accessible routes are code-compliant. On projects targeting LEED or other green certifications, I'm also working with ASHRAE 90.1 or the Florida Energy Code for water heating efficiency.*

*And I always start a project by confirming which code edition the local jurisdiction has adopted and checking for any local amendments. Tampa, for example, sometimes has specific requirements that go beyond the baseline state code."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Lists core codes**: IPC, IBC, FBC, NFPA 99, ADA, ICC A117.1, ASHRAE 90.1
- ✅ **Explains when each is used**: shows understanding, not just memorization
- ✅ **Mentions local amendments**: demonstrates real-world awareness
- ✅ **Proactive**: confirms edition and local requirements upfront

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Just listing codes without context
- ❌ Forgetting healthcare-specific codes (NFPA 99) if healthcare is part of the firm's work
- ❌ Ignoring accessibility codes (critical compliance area)

### **18) How do you handle accessibility requirements (ADA/FBC) in plumbing design?**

**Example Answer:**

*"Accessibility starts early in the design process. I work with the architect to understand the occupancy and use, which determines how many accessible fixtures are required. For example, a certain percentage of toilet rooms have to be fully accessible, and within those rooms you need accessible water closets, lavatories, and sometimes accessible bathing fixtures.*

*For accessible water closets, I make sure there's adequate clearance for wheelchair approach and transfer—typically 60 inches of turning space, specific side and front clearances, and grab bars at the right heights and locations. I coordinate with the architect on grab bar blocking in the walls.*

*Lavatories need to be mounted at the right height—typically 34 inches to the top of the rim—with knee clearance underneath, insulated or shielded hot water piping to prevent burns, and lever or automatic controls. Faucets can't require tight grasping or twisting.*

*I also pay attention to accessible routes to the plumbing fixtures—door clearances, turning radii, and threshold heights. And for facilities like bathing rooms or kitchens, there are additional requirements for clear floor space, controls, and maneuvering space.*

*I use the ICC A117.1 standard as my primary reference because it has detailed diagrams and dimensions, and it's referenced by the IBC and FBC. I also review the ADA Standards for Accessible Design to make sure we're meeting federal requirements, especially for government or public projects."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Shows early coordination**: works with architect from the start
- ✅ **Specific dimensions and clearances**: 60" turning, 34" lav height, knee clearance
- ✅ **Mentions grab bars and coordination**: blocking, structural coordination
- ✅ **Addresses controls and safety**: lever handles, insulated piping
- ✅ **Names reference standards**: ICC A117.1, ADA (shows you know where to look)

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Vague answer ("I follow ADA")
- ❌ Not mentioning specific clearances or dimensions (shows lack of detailed knowledge)
- ❌ Ignoring coordination with architect (fixtures don't exist in isolation)
- ❌ Forgetting grab bar blocking or pipe insulation (common oversights)

## **Part 5: Behavioral & Problem-Solving**

### **20) Tell us about a challenge and how you dealt with it.**

**Example Answer:**

*"On a mid-rise office building renovation, we were upgrading the restrooms on multiple floors, and during demolition the contractor discovered that the existing sanitary stack was severely corroded and needed to be replaced. The problem was that the building was occupied, and we couldn't shut down the restrooms for more than a few hours at a time.*

*I was responsible for finding a solution that would allow us to replace the stack without a prolonged shutdown. I proposed a phased approach where we'd install a new sanitary stack in an adjacent chase, tie it in floor-by-floor during off-hours and weekends, and then cut over each floor's drainage to the new stack in sequence. That way only one floor was affected at a time, and only for a few hours.*

*I worked with the structural engineer to confirm the new chase could accommodate the stack and that we could penetrate the slabs. I coordinated with the facilities manager to schedule the work during low-occupancy periods, and I designed temporary bypass piping for critical floors that couldn't be down at all.*

*The solution added about two weeks to the schedule and some cost for the temporary work, but it was far better than shutting down half the building for a month. The building owner was very happy with the approach, and we completed the work with minimal disruption. Tenants barely noticed."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Clear STAR structure**: Situation (corroded stack, occupied building) → Task (replace without long shutdown) → Action (phased replacement, coordination, temporary bypass) → Result (minimal disruption, owner satisfaction)
- ✅ **Shows problem-solving**: phased approach, temporary bypass piping
- ✅ **Demonstrates coordination**: structural (new penetrations), facilities (scheduling), contractor
- ✅ **Quantifies impact**: two weeks added vs. one month shutdown avoided
- ✅ **Client focus**: tenant satisfaction, owner approval

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Blaming others (contractor, original designer, building owner)
- ❌ Describing the problem without explaining the solution
- ❌ Vague outcome ("we figured it out")

### **22) How do you prioritize when multiple projects are due at once?**

**Example Answer:**

*"I start by mapping out the hard deadlines—permit submittal dates, bid packages, construction milestones, 50% review, 100% CDs—and I work backward to figure out how much time I realistically need for each deliverable. Then I communicate with the project managers to make sure we're aligned on the schedule and flag any conflicts early.*

*If I have competing deadlines, I look for opportunities to stagger them by a few days, or I ask if any have flexibility. Most PMs are willing to work with you if you're transparent early, rather than waiting until the last minute and creating a crisis.*

*I also batch similar tasks when I can—if I'm doing water demand calculations for three projects, I'll knock them all out in one session while I'm in that mindset. It's more efficient than jumping between totally different task types.*

*And if I'm genuinely overloaded to the point where quality or deadlines are at risk, I escalate to my supervisor early. I've learned it's better to ask for help a week out than to miss a deadline or deliver subpar work and let the team down."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Systematic approach**: map deadlines, work backward, communicate proactively
- ✅ **Proactive communication**: aligns with PMs, asks for flexibility when needed
- ✅ **Efficiency thinking**: batches similar tasks to minimize context-switching
- ✅ **Self-awareness**: knows when to escalate rather than martyring through impossible situations

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ "I just work harder" (unsustainable, not strategic)
- ❌ Claiming they've never had deadline conflicts (unrealistic)
- ❌ Implying they handle everything alone without communication
- ❌ No mention of escalation when truly needed

### **24) Tell me about a project that went wrong—what did you learn?**

**Example Answer:**

*"Early in my career, I was designing the plumbing for a small commercial building, and I undersized the water heater because I used a rule-of-thumb estimate instead of doing a proper hot water demand calculation. The contractor installed it per my drawings, but during commissioning the owner complained that the hot water recovery was too slow during peak use.*

*We ended up having to add a second water heater in parallel, which worked, but it was a change order that cost the owner money and delayed the certificate of occupancy by about a week. The owner was frustrated, and my PM was not happy with me.*

*What I learned was to never rely on rules of thumb for equipment sizing—always do the actual calculation, especially for critical equipment like water heaters, pumps, and tanks. I also learned to review manufacturer's performance data and recovery rates, not just storage capacity.*

*Since then, I've built a proper hot water demand calculation spreadsheet that I use on every project, and I've never had a similar issue. I also make a point of reviewing equipment selections with a senior engineer before they go into the drawings, especially on building types I haven't done before."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Honest and specific**: owns the mistake (undersized water heater, didn't calc properly)
- ✅ **Explains the impact**: change order cost, schedule delay, unhappy client and PM
- ✅ **Clear lesson learned**: always calculate, don't rely on rules of thumb
- ✅ **Shows process improvement**: built calculation tool, added senior review step
- ✅ **Demonstrates growth**: hasn't repeated the mistake

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Blaming the contractor, manufacturer, or client
- ❌ Downplaying the mistake ("it was no big deal")
- ❌ Vague lesson ("I'll be more careful")
- ❌ Not showing how behavior changed

### **27) How do you perform clash detection and resolve coordination issues?**

**Example Answer:**

*"On projects that use BIM, I model the plumbing systems in Revit MEP and participate in regular coordination meetings where we run clash detection in Navisworks. The software identifies hard clashes—places where my plumbing pipes physically intersect with structure, ductwork, electrical conduit, sprinkler piping, or other systems.*

*I prioritize the clashes based on severity. Structural conflicts and code-required clearances are top priority—like maintaining proper distances from electrical panels or ensuring adequate drainage slope. Then I work through routing optimization and aesthetic conflicts.*

*For each clash, we discuss in the coordination meeting who can move what. Plumbing often has some routing flexibility, especially for branches, but I advocate when a particular route is critical—like when I need gravity fall for drainage or when I'm trying to minimize pressure drop on a long water run.*

*We document all the resolutions in the meeting minutes and update our models so everyone is working from coordinated information. On one project, we identified and resolved over 80 clashes before construction, which the GC told us saved at least two weeks of field coordination time and prevented multiple RFIs.*

*On projects without BIM, I do the same process manually—overlaying discipline drawings, checking sections and elevations, and raising coordination questions in meetings. It takes longer, but the principle is the same."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Names specific tools**: Revit MEP, Navisworks
- ✅ **Explains the process**: model → run clash detection → prioritize → coordinate → document → update
- ✅ **Shows judgment**: knows when plumbing can flex vs. when to advocate
- ✅ **Quantifies value**: 80 clashes resolved, 2 weeks saved
- ✅ **Acknowledges non-BIM projects**: shows adaptability

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ "I just run clash detection and send the report" (no ownership of resolution)
- ❌ Not mentioning prioritization (lacks critical thinking)
- ❌ Suggesting plumbing always moves (poor advocacy for your discipline)

## **Part 7: Energy Efficiency & Sustainability**

### **30) How do you approach water efficiency and sustainable plumbing design?**

**Example Answer:**

*"Water efficiency starts with fixture selection. I specify WaterSense-labeled fixtures wherever possible—low-flow faucets at 0.5 GPM or less, high-efficiency toilets at 1.28 GPF or dual-flush, and low-flow showerheads. Those are easy wins that save water without sacrificing performance.*

*For water heating, I look at high-efficiency options—tankless water heaters where the load profile makes sense, heat pump water heaters for certain applications, or solar thermal preheat systems when the building orientation and roof space allow it. I also design hot water recirculation systems carefully—using demand controls or timers instead of continuous circulation, and making sure the system is properly balanced and insulated to minimize heat loss.*

*On projects targeting LEED or other green certifications, I sometimes design rainwater harvesting or graywater reuse systems. Rainwater is typically used for irrigation or toilet flushing, and graywater from lavatories and showers can be treated and reused for toilets or cooling tower makeup. Those systems require careful coordination with health departments and code officials because they're not universally accepted yet, but they can dramatically reduce potable water use.*

*I also pay attention to insulation—both for energy conservation and condensation control. And when I'm designing outdoor plumbing or irrigation systems, I try to use native or drought-tolerant landscaping strategies that reduce irrigation demand in the first place."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Covers multiple strategies**: low-flow fixtures, high-efficiency heating, recirculation controls, alternative water systems, insulation
- ✅ **Specific numbers**: 0.5 GPM faucets, 1.28 GPF toilets (shows you know the standards)
- ✅ **Demonstrates code/certification knowledge**: WaterSense, LEED, health department coordination
- ✅ **Systems thinking**: reduce demand first, then improve efficiency
- ✅ **Practical awareness**: notes that alternative systems "aren't universally accepted yet"

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Vague answer ("I use efficient fixtures")
- ❌ Ignoring water heating (major energy use)
- ❌ Not mentioning alternative water systems if the firm does green buildings
- ❌ Forgetting insulation and controls (critical for actual performance)

## **Part 8: Specialized Systems**

### **33) Have you designed domestic hot water systems? Walk me through your approach.**

**Example Answer:**

*"Yes, I design hot water systems on almost every project. I start by calculating the hot water demand, which depends on the building type. For residential or hospitality, I might use the fixture count and storage/draw method from ASHRAE or the manufacturer's sizing guides. For commercial buildings, I typically use a fixture unit method or a per-occupant estimation.*

*Next, I select the water heater type. For smaller buildings with steady demand, tank-type heaters work well—gas or electric depending on utility availability and operating cost. For buildings with high peak demand but low average use—like athletic facilities or schools—I might use high-recovery heaters or a combination of storage tanks and instantaneous heaters. For constant high demand, like hospitals or hotels, I often use boiler-fed storage tanks with heat exchangers.*

*I also design the recirculation system to maintain temperature at the fixtures without long wait times. I use a recirculation pump, balancing valves on each branch to ensure even flow, and a temperature sensor and aquastat to control the pump. Ideally, I use a demand control or timer to minimize energy waste during low-use periods.*

*Temperature is critical—I typically design for 120°F at fixtures for safety and energy efficiency, but some applications like commercial dishwashers need 140°F or higher, so I use point-of-use boosters or separate systems. I also consider Legionella prevention—avoiding dead legs, maintaining temperature above 120°F in the distribution system, and designing for periodic flushing.*

*Finally, I size expansion tanks, pressure relief valves, and tempering valves as needed for safety and code compliance."*

**Why This Works:**
- ✅ **Covers full design process**: demand calculation → equipment selection → recirculation → temperature control → safety devices
- ✅ **Shows adaptability**: different approaches for different building types and load profiles
- ✅ **Demonstrates code/safety knowledge**: Legionella prevention, temperature limits, PRVs
- ✅ **Explains controls**: demand control, timers, balancing valves (not just "install a pump")
- ✅ **Addresses special cases**: dishwasher boosters, hospitals

**What Was Avoided:**
- ❌ Oversimplifying ("I just size a water heater")
- ❌ Ignoring recirculation (critical for large buildings)
- ❌ Not mentioning temperature control or Legionella (health/safety issues)
- ❌ Forgetting expansion tanks and safety devices

## **Final Interview Tips**

### **The 3-Second Rule**
Pause 2-3 seconds after they finish asking before you answer. This:
- Prevents interrupting if they're still talking
- Gives you time to organize your thoughts
- Makes you appear thoughtful, not reactive

### **The 2-Minute Rule**
Most answers should be 60-120 seconds. Longer and you risk losing attention. If you're rambling, wrap up with: *"Happy to go deeper on any part of that if useful."*

### **The Specificity Principle**
Specific beats generic every time:
- ❌ "I worked on many buildings"
- ✅ "I designed plumbing for a 15-story office tower, a 100-bed hospital, and a research lab"

### **The Positivity Filter**
Even when discussing challenges, stay constructive:
- ❌ "My last project was a disaster because the architect kept changing things"
- ✅ "One challenging project had frequent design changes, which taught me to build more coordination checkpoints into the schedule"

### **The Confidence Calibration**
- Don't say "I think" or "I believe" about things you know:
  - ❌ "I think IPC requires 1/4" slope for drainage"
  - ✅ "IPC requires 1/4" slope for horizontal drains up to 3 inches"
- But DO acknowledge what you don't know:
  - ✅ "I haven't designed a graywater reuse system yet, but that's something I'm eager to learn"
